*Extracted from the Science Directorate Field Manual, Vol. II: Capacity and Rating. Distribution: general.*

Every caster draws [the Weave](/arcanum/the-weave) from an internal reserve the Directorate terms the mana well — a biological resource, analogous to adrenaline or metabolic energy, present in every person without exception. The metaphor is chosen with care: a well has a depth, a refill rate, and a bottom, and a caster who forgets any of the three digs their own grave with the fourth working of the afternoon.

## Two Variables, Not One

Magical capacity is governed by two independent biological factors, and the Directorate insists recruits stop conflating them.

- **Access efficiency** — whether a person falls within the population capable of martial magical use at all. Talent and training develop precise access; not everyone can reach it regardless of effort.
- **Well size** — entirely separate, and the reason the same technique produces a hot stream of flame from a standard well and a steel-melting inferno from a large one. The physics application is identical; the scale is not. An enemy cannot assess what they face by recognizing the technique — only the first cast reveals the well behind it.

Large wells are not an Army monopoly. Civilians, foreign soldiers, and ungeared practitioners can possess them. What the Grand Army supplies is the training and runic infrastructure to use a large well safely and to its full potential — without which a large well is as likely to kill its owner through unmanaged heat as to be an asset. [Sovereign's Arcanum](/grand-army/purple-guard) members almost universally sit at the extreme large end of the natural distribution; the Violet Trials are calibrated to demand both the technique and the well capacity to sustain it through a prolonged engagement. Perfect technique in a small well fails the Trials as surely as a large well with poor technique.

## Measurement

Well capacity is measured at the [Imperial College](/geography/caeldrion) against the standard threshold scale, first through twelfth. The survey standard is an [Engineering Corps](/grand-army/engineering-corps) instrument, recalibrated annually, and the rating is entered in the soldier's record beside marksmanship and drill — a capacity, not a virtue.

- **Thresholds one through four** — the common range; sufficient for trade-weaving and utility work.
- **Five through seven** — field-caster range; relay and support casting under supervision.
- **Eighth and above** — command-weaving range. A stable well above the seventh threshold is one of the two gates into the [Cobalt Ascendancy](/grand-army/cobalt-ascendancy).

## Well Development: The Muscle Model

A well is not a static asset. Like physical musculature, it responds to use and neglect over years, with consequences that follow a soldier from the Crucible to the retirement roll.

- **Training and growth** — consistent practice develops the well, increasing both accessible capacity and, over sufficient time, its actual size. The improvement is real and measurable. It is also gradual: there is no shortcut, and attempts to accelerate it through overextension damage the well rather than grow it.
- **Atrophy** — a well left unused diminishes over time. Retired soldiers who stop practicing lose capacity gradually, and veterans returning to active service after a long gap find their wells regressed and must rebuild them. The [Medical Corps'](/grand-army/medical-corps) Fourth Seat tracks long-term well health for career soldiers; the Fifth Seat assesses the psychological toll of well atrophy on retired Arcanum members, for whom the loss of magical capacity is a genuine identity adjustment.
- **Catastrophic damage** — overextension, pushing through Stage 2 [Burn](/arcanum/the-burn) repeatedly, or attempting Tier IV output without adequate armor or well capacity, damages the well permanently if severe enough. A soldier who survives a Mana Collapse event, or who repeatedly reaches Stage 2 without recovery, may find their capacity permanently reduced — the most severe long-term consequence of poor output management, and the primary reason the Medical Corps monitors career soldiers' well health throughout their service.

## Depletion

A well does not empty politely. Draw-down past the safe margin presents as tremor, tunnel vision, and pattern collapse — and a collapsing pattern dumps its remaining charge as [Burn](/arcanum/the-burn). Field doctrine therefore rates casters not by their maximum but by their *sustainable* draw, and every relay team logs expenditure by the working.

## Casting Without Armor

[Runic armor](/arcanum/runic-armor) manages the heat generated by magical output; without it, that heat accumulates at the well's unmanaged discharge rate. Consequence scales sharply with output tier — the same four-tier scale used throughout this Archive to classify the magnitude of an effect, detailed in full in [the Burn](/arcanum/the-burn).

| Output tier | Unarmored consequence |
| --- | --- |
| Tier I | Essentially free — negligible heat, sustainable indefinitely |
| Tier II | Uncomfortable but manageable for a limited period; degraded and taxing for hours after |
| Tier III | A single cast is the practical limit — one effect pushes toward Stage 1, sometimes Stage 2, Burn |
| Tier IV | Unsurvivable without extraordinary well size and resilience, and dangerous even then |

Armor loss is therefore a genuine tactical crisis for any specialist whose role requires Tier III output — a Cobalt weaver without gauntlets, a Vanguard without a runic weapon, can still fight but cannot sustain the output that defines their division's capability. Soldiers who expect to need a backup carry [runic body inscription](/arcanum/runic-body-work) as insurance against exactly this scenario.

## Training

Depth is largely fixed by nature; discipline is not. The College's regimen trains refill efficiency and draw economy — the same well, spent three times as well. The Directorate's motto for the course is stitched into every training doublet: *the deep well brags, the managed well wins*.

> Directorate note: claims of bottomless wells appear in this Archive exactly as often as their claimants appear in the Medical Corps' Burn ward, and the correlation is not considered mysterious.

*War Academy Case Study #409 — General Educational Release. Unit: Squadron 7. Composition: Cpt. Valerius (Cobalt Maestro), Sgt. Forge (Crimson Vanguard), Scout Elara ([Green Ranger](/grand-army/green-rangers) Corps), Specialists Rivet and Mason (Engineering Corps), and six Legionnaires ([Iron Legion](/grand-army/iron-legion)). Threat: Rot Rebel Druids and a Class 5 Highland Scorch-Drake. Lessons: five-color synergy, thermal management, inter-rank respect, Purple Guard field doctrine.*

> Archivist's note: Scout Elara of the Green Ranger Corps, present in this action, is a different individual from [Lady Elara, the Silent Maestro](/history/the-ascendants) of the Purple Guard. The Academy's case file makes the distinction explicitly; this Archive preserves it for the same reason.

## The Squadron

Squadron 7 was, by the case file's own description, an ordinary composite unit rather than a hand-picked showcase — a Cobalt Captain, a Vanguard sergeant, a Ranger scout, two Engineering specialists, and six Legionnaires, the same five-color mixture the [Grand Army](/grand-army) fields in hundreds of comparable units across the Empire's frontier. The Academy selected this file for the curriculum precisely because nothing about the squadron's composition was exceptional. What the engagement tested was whether an ordinary unit, correctly trained across five disciplines, could survive a threat well beyond its rated capacity — and whether the [Purple Guard's](/grand-army/purple-guard) intervention, when it came, would reinforce that unit's own competence or simply replace it.

## The Ambush

The engagement began not with steel but with earth. A Rot Druid "Liquefy" spell converted the hard-packed road beneath Squadron 7 to watery slurry; six Iron Legionnaires sank to their waists, their plate dragging them down. They triggered Brace Runes immediately, locking into immovable statues — cemented in a kill zone, but alive.

Captain Valerius ordered an Engineering response while deflecting necrotic bolts. Specialists Mason and Rivet waded into the chaos and, deploying binding magic through the mud, converted the liquefied sinkhole into a rigid stone trench in seconds. The Legionnaires stayed anchored, but now in cover rather than a grave.

Scout Elara worked from the canopy without a clear line of sight, solving the problem with atmospheric weaving — three arrows banked around a massive oak tree on guided air currents, striking three hidden archers without exposing her position.

Sergeant Forge's maul cracked under a lucky enemy strike mid-engagement. Specialist Rivet performed a Combat Fusion repair under fire, grabbing the superheated damaged steel bare-handed and fusing the crack shut with binding magic in seconds, and Forge answered with a Thermal Dump that vaporized the enemy's Stone Construct entirely.

## The Intervention

The squad had won the engagement. Then the sky went dark, the temperature dropped ten degrees, and a Highland Scorch-Drake — forty feet of scale and malice — landed on the ridge, three Rot-Hulks crashing through the treeline behind it. The squad was combat-ineffective: Forge's armor glowed cherry-red at dangerous heat, Valerius was out of mana, the Legionnaires were still cemented in the trench. The Drake inhaled, the back of its throat glowing blinding white.

There was no roar. Instead: the rhythmic clack of hooves on stone. A lone rider in Diplomatic Configuration — mirror-bright war-steel plate trimmed in real gold, a heavy purple velvet cloak — trotted from the treeline. Lord Aris of the [Purple Guard](/grand-army/purple-guard).

The Drake's fire torrent hit an invisible wall five feet from the squad and vanished, the heat shunted instantly into the upper atmosphere through the Pillar of the Aegis. Aris dismounted; one frame he was on the ground, the next he stood on the Drake's neck, driving a simple dagger into its skull — a Void Application that opened a singularity for a fraction of a second and collapsed the creature's brainstem into nothing. The forty-foot beast went limp instantly. He turned to the three Rot-Hulks, and a flick of his wrist lifted all three simultaneously with Mass Telekinesis and slammed them into each other. Silence returned to the Weald.

## The After-Action Review

Lord Aris did not depart. He walked directly into the mud, gold-trimmed boots sinking into the muck, and approached the trench.

He pulled a Black Private to his feet: "Solid brace, Trooper. The Anvil did not break."

To Sergeant Forge: "Clean detonation on the construct. Your heat management is aggressive, but effective. Drink water."

To Specialist Mason: "The structural integrity was excellent. You saved their lives. However — the curing time was a fraction slow. Next time, try pulsing a thermal spike before the binding rune. It sets the matrix instantly. We use that technique in the Palace walls."

He remounted, and offered Captain Valerius a sharp, crisp salute — a peer acknowledging a peer. "Good command, Captain. The road is clear."

> Archivist's note: cadets studying this file sometimes observe that its Captain Valerius shares a name with the current Heir Apparent, [Prince Valerius Caelaris](/icons/the-royal-family), who held an active Cobalt commission as a Captain in the years before his brother's Ascension. The Archive neither confirms nor denies the identification. The file was sealed under a standard designation before the question became worth asking, and it has not been reopened since.

## The Named Lessons

The case file's classification table names four lessons explicitly, and the Academy examines cadets on all four separately before moving to the After-Action Review:

- **Five-Color Synergy** — no single color of the [Sea of Silver](/grand-army) won this engagement. The Legion's Brace Runes bought time; Engineering's binding magic turned a grave into cover; a Ranger's atmospheric weaving solved a problem no line soldier could see; a Vanguard's Thermal Dump finished what the others had set up. Squadron 7 survived because six different disciplines each did the one thing only they could do, in the correct sequence.
- **Thermal Management** — Sergeant Forge's armor glowing cherry-red before the Drake even landed is the case file's cautionary half of the lesson: aggressive heat output wins engagements and costs the body doing it. Lord Aris's own field note — "drink water" — is not a joke in the transcript. It is standard [Runic Armor](/arcanum/runic-armor) doctrine on managing the Burn, delivered in the only register a exhausted sergeant mid-engagement can actually absorb.
- **Inter-Rank Respect** — detailed below in the After-Action Review itself.
- **Purple Guard Field Doctrine** — a Guard's intervention does not end an engagement's chain of command. Lord Aris solved the threat the squadron could not solve alone, then handed authority straight back to Captain Valerius rather than assuming it. The Guard reinforces the Anvil. It does not replace it.

## Instructor's Note

Cadets are directed to pay particular attention to the After-Action Review Lord Aris conducted on the field. He did not dismiss the squad. He offered specific technical correction to the Engineering Specialist and validated the Iron Legion's defensive hold, in the same breath and the same tone.

- **The Purple Guard are not separate from the Army** — they are the peak of it. Their duty is to uplift the lower ranks, not disdain them.
- **Constructive Elite** — a Purple Guard who ignores a flaw in a lower rank is failing their duty. One who corrects it ensures the Empire is stronger tomorrow.
- **Respect the Anvil** — Lord Aris saluted the Captain and the Privates alike. In the Grand Army, the color of the cloth denotes function, not human worth. All bleed the same red. All serve the same Sovereign.

## Why the Weald, and Why It Stayed in the Curriculum

The Academy has considered retiring Case Study #409 more than once — it is decades old, and newer engagements exist. It has not been retired, because no later file demonstrates the same lesson as cleanly: a squadron that did everything correctly and would still have died without the [Purple Guard's](/grand-army/purple-guard) intervention, met by a Guard who intervened precisely and then left without taking anything from the squadron that was rightfully theirs — not the credit, not the command, not the story. Cadets who study Lord Aris's after-action review often ask what they would have said in his place. The Academy's honest answer is that most would have said less, and meant it less specifically. The file endures because the standard it sets is still, by the Academy's own admission, aspirational.

> Archivist's note: Case Study #409 is required reading before a cadet's first field posting. The Academy has found no better single document for teaching what rank is actually for.
